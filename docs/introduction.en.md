# The Argument as Written, and the Argument at Work — A Plain Introduction

*[한국어](introduction.md) | English*

> The deeper theory (what truth is, what "layers" are) is gathered separately in the [appendix](appendix_deep.en.md). This piece keeps to one thing, in plain terms: why a tool like this is needed.

---

## 1. We don't see and then believe

Once you dislike someone, everything they do looks bad. Once someone looks like a fraud to you, even their excuses look like tricks. We call this being petty, but really it's just how the human mind works.

Our brains don't see the world as it is. We filter it through the framework of beliefs we already hold. So when we meet a piece of evidence, we don't ask "if I were wrong, would this look different?" — we ask "does this fit what I already think?" And almost any piece of evidence fits almost any belief. Unpaid money looks like proof of fraud — but someone whose business simply failed also can't pay. Evidence that fits piles up; evidence that doesn't just gets one extra explanation: "oh, that's a cover." So with every piece of evidence, the belief only grows stronger.

The bigger problem is that **belief even decides what we go looking for.** If "fraud" comes first, we hunt for things that fit fraud and never look for the circumstances that don't. What you never looked for becomes, in effect, nonexistent — which makes this bias the hardest of all to catch later.

This isn't laziness or dishonesty. It's the same circuit running in everyone's head. And the circuit isn't broken — it's **a successful design.** An animal that weighs "tiger or wind?" fairly when the bushes rustle gets eaten mid-deliberation. The ones who jumped to conclusions survived. The catch: long ago, a wrong call was corrected within seconds (no tiger, just a wasted flinch). But modern judgments — trials, investigations, investments, policy — give back their results years later, or never. **The fast judgment is still there; only the fast feedback that used to correct it is gone.**

So "just be unbiased" is empty advice — it's asking us to undo evolution. Only two things are actually possible: **make visible which belief is making this evidence look the way it does,** and rebuild, as an outside **procedure**, the thing the bushes used to do — push back quickly on wrong guesses.

## 2. Why take arguments apart

Other people's conclusions come from the same circuit. News articles, reports, advertisements, investment pitches, online posts — what reaches us is always a smooth, finished surface. But the real work that made the conclusion happened off the page: which facts were picked and which were dropped, what assumption silently bridged the gap from evidence to conclusion, which questions were never even asked.

> "They took the crowdfunding pledges and paid off other debt. Therefore they meant to run off with it from the start."

This stands on an **unwritten sentence**: "A normal creator wouldn't spend pledged money on anything else." While it stays hidden, it slips by as obvious. Pull it out, and it becomes a claim you can test — is that really true? What does a creator do when the money runs dry?

Taking an argument apart isn't nitpicking — it's **bringing these hidden bridges into the open.** Because conclusions are really decided not by the written sentences but by the unwritten assumptions, the alternative explanations never raised, the evidence never gathered.

Here's the paradox: **the most dangerous writing isn't the sloppy kind, it's the smooth kind.** Anyone can spot a sloppy flaw. But writing whose conclusion was fixed before the evidence has nowhere to wobble — the more consistent it is, the less it gets questioned. A smooth story is not proof of truth. Sometimes it's just proof of good editing.

This structure is easiest to see outside the courtroom. Look at Holocaust denial, or creationism. Both come with huge evidence lists and tidy narratives — but the structure is identical. **No piece of evidence ever changes the conclusion:** if survivor testimony and records are produced, "they're exaggerated or forged"; if a transitional fossil turns up, "that dating method is suspect." Each inconvenient fact just gets one more patch, and to a believer the theory only looks sturdier. The decisive thing is the **question never asked**: "If I were right, what evidence would have to exist — and did I actually go looking for it?"

And this doesn't stay inside one person's head. Picture a society — in the US, in Korea, anywhere — where fake news and "fake analysis" run rampant (analysis that looks rigorous with data and logic on the outside but is hollow within). When claims that no evidence can shake fill the public square, the shared floor of "what counts as grounds" disappears. Democracy is a system that depends on rival camps meeting and being reconciled in front of the same facts; once that common floor collapses, debate stops being persuasion and becomes the shouting of camps. When confirmation bias becomes not a personal flaw but a society's default, that is also how a democracy comes apart.

That is exactly why it matters that this test **doesn't pick sides.** The same questions apply just as hard to the opposing claim. What the test reveals isn't "true or false" but **structure** — a claim built so that no evidence can shake it isn't telling you about the world, only about how it was built.

So here is what argument analysis is really after. Not reflecting on *every* premise — that's impossible, and the act of reflecting just stands on further premises. It's surfacing the premises that **actually change the conclusion**, so that reality can test them. As long as a premise stays hidden, I mistake my own conclusion for reality; the moment it's out in the open, reality can push back on a wrong idea and correct me. Not perfect reflection, but **a state that can be corrected** — that's the whole aim. (And it matters most where results come back slowly — trials, investments, policy — because there a wrong premise hardens before reality ever corrects it.)

## 3. The AI age — why it matters more now

AI now produces smooth writing without limit, almost for free. Consistent narratives, plausible arguments, tidy reports — cheaper than ever. In an age when smoothness is free, the habit of "it's smooth, so it must be right" turns dangerous. **The eye that tells "sounds right" from "is right"** is the new literacy, the one needed right after reading and writing.

And the problem doesn't stop there.

**AI tries to take our side.** Built to be agreeable and fluent, it will, when asked to "defend my view," compose a beautiful defense. Not neutral writing, but a more polished version of what you already lean toward — handed back to you. It doesn't shake your blind spot; it hardens it.

**AI is confidently wrong.** It speaks with the same authority whether its grounds are solid or empty, and it will fabricate a fact as fluently as it states a true one. So "confident and well-written" is no longer evidence of being grounded.

**Producing got cheap; checking didn't.** AI collapsed only the cost of *making* claims, not the cost of *examining* them. So the world fills with claims faster than we can scrutinize them.

So what should the machine do, and what should the human do?

**There are things humans can't do:** building every hidden premise across hundreds of pages without missing one, asking every claim the same question with the same force, tirelessly listing the evidence the other side would have sought. This isn't a matter of diligence but of the mental structure from Part 1 — willpower won't fix it. And a flood of claims has to be met at the machine's speed. **This job belongs to the machine.**

**There are things machines can't do:** choosing what's the heart of the case, deciding which evidence to trust, and taking responsibility for that judgment. When AI makes nearly everything abundant, the thing that turns scarce is neither information nor fluent reasoning but exactly this — **judgment.** Whoever hands their judgment to a machine has outsourced their thinking; whoever hands the laying-out to a machine has outsourced their limitations. The first is dangerous; the second is wisdom.

So this tool doesn't switch AI off — it **turns AI against its own grain.** Not "take my side," but "lay out what my claim stands on, and what the other side would have looked for." The machine doesn't manufacture doubt — it **marks where doubt belongs** — and the human decides which spot to stand on and lives with it. It turns the very smoothness AI floods us with into a mirror that reflects us back.

## 4. LARP (Layer-grounded Argument Reasoning Probe) — the tool that turns this idea into a procedure

LARP turns the idea above into an actual procedure. Feed in a news article, a report, any piece of writing, and the tool takes its argument apart and draws it as **a single map.** The map shows not just what's written (conclusions, claims, evidence) but, in dotted lines, what isn't: the hidden premises that silently bridge gaps, the alternative explanations never raised, the evidence that should exist if those alternatives were true but is missing, and an opinion on whether the whole document leans toward its conclusion.

After drawing the map, the tool **stops.** Where to dig deeper is the human's call. It analyzes only the spots you point to, and whatever doubt remains is left as a list: "what to check, how, and what it would mean if confirmed." **The machine lays everything out to the end; the judgment stays the human's to the end.**

---

*Seeing evidence through what you already believe is something everyone already does. Correcting your beliefs to fit the evidence is a skill that must be learned. The name of that skill is taking arguments apart — pulling out the assumptions working beneath the written sentences, and making them something you can question. Don't follow the name; follow the conditions.*
