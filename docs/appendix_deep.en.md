# Appendix — Going Deeper

*[한국어](appendix_deep.md) | English*

> Read this appendix **only if you want to.** You don't need it to use the tool. We suggest reading the [introduction](introduction.en.md) first.
> Here we set out the three ideas LARP stands on. As plainly as possible — but a step deeper than the introduction.

---

## 1. What truth is — "where conditions meet"

People usually imagine truth as one of two things: bending the world to fit your words, or copying the world exactly while you yourself vanish. LARP says it's neither.

Truth is **what comes about when the question you pose and the response the world gives meet, within present conditions.** You form a hypothesis and look at evidence, but the world doesn't follow your words. Some evidence supports your view, some resists it, and some can't separate the options at all.

So **the real value of evidence lies in its power to pull competing explanations apart.** Evidence that fits both explanations, however much of it there is, can't tell you which one is true. (In the earlier example, "he didn't repay" was like that — fraud and a failed business both lead to non-repayment.) This is called *diagnosticity*.

From this comes the first principle: **look at conditions, not names.** No object is a fixed essence; it's a bundle of conditions. Gather the conditions and it appears; remove them and it's gone; change them and it becomes something else. The name "fraudster" should not be the starting conclusion but the **result** of checking the conditions.

One more thing. Even the same sentence needs different evidence depending on **what is being asked:**
- Is it so now? (e.g. "he has no ability to repay now")
- Was it so in the past? (e.g. "he meant to cheat when he took the money")
- Will it be so? (e.g. "can his promise to repay be trusted")

In a criminal case the crux is almost always **was it so in the past** — and the most common mistake is using **what is so now** (currently broke) as if it were evidence of the past.

---

## 2. Conditions come in layers

Conditions don't sit side by side on one flat plane. Facts, the meaning of words, the standard for "same," evidence, legal requirements, emotions, actions, timing… for the same case, **which layer you look through** changes what's true, what evidence you need, and what would shake it. LARP calls these layers, and a single piece of data can sit on several at once.

But not every layer is equally useful. To be a **useful layer**, four things must be present: a condition for it to hold, evidence to confirm it, a way to show it wrong, and an action it calls for. A layer missing these four isn't a "perspective" — it's just **consolation.** A good layer changes what you'd do and exposes where you could be wrong; a bad one only makes you feel better while verifying nothing. (Covering one layer with another is "kidding yourself"; seeing the layers as they are is "seeing clearly.")

There's a small loop here. **To know what the object is, you need to know the observer's standpoint; to know the standpoint, you need to know the object.** You don't break this loop — you **make it visible through contrast.** Set up an opposing standpoint, and how the two arrange the same facts differently *is* the outline of the standpoint. (That's why LARP makes you write, for every important argument, "if I were the other side, where would I place this, and what evidence would I have sought?")

---

## 3. What a "problem argument" is — "the gap from the reconstruction"

An argument is not a list of propositions. It's **the act of a person with a standpoint crossing from grounds to a conclusion while fending off competing explanations.** So an argument as written is always an **incomplete surface** — the connecting premise is left out, alternatives go unmentioned, ungathered evidence leaves no trace, and the writer's standpoint is dissolved into how the material is arranged.

So verification can only be **reconstruction.** Rather than hunting for flaws on the surface, you first rebuild the complete argument and then see where the original departs from it. In two directions.

- **Forward:** what would have to be true for this inference to hold? → pull out the most charitable premise.
- **Backward:** what conditions does this claim actually stand on? → trace the reason that really did the work.

The **point where these two split** — claiming "common sense" on the surface while actually "reading the outcome backwards" — is the strongest signal.

So **the definition of a "problem argument" is not "a wrong argument."** Among the gaps between the reconstructed complete argument and the surface, a problem argument is **the gap you must resolve before accepting that conclusion at that strength.** It isn't declaring something false; it's marking the spots that are dangerous to wave through.

The most dangerous, again, is **smooth writing.** A document whose conclusion was set before the evidence has nowhere to wobble, so it passes every individual check. That's why you must ask the document as a whole — do the hidden premises point one way, is evidence that fits both sides used as the core, does any record keep the conclusion intact? **If all three signals point the same way, the document may be a record of defense, not of discovery.**

---

## 4. The human and the machine

The analyst can't stand outside the world either — and neither can an AI. So the work gets divided.

**What the machine takes on:** what humans can't do because of how the mind is built — building every hidden premise without missing one, asking every claim the same question with the same force, tirelessly listing the evidence the other side would have sought.

**What the human takes on:** what machines can't do in principle — choosing what's the crux, deciding what to trust, and taking responsibility for that judgment.

The machine marks where doubt belongs; the human decides which of those spots to stand on, and bears it. Finding truth isn't one side's job — it happens **where a machine that won't close the field meets a human who won't dodge the responsibility of closing it.**
